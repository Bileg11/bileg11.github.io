02 — Student Visa
X1 and X2 Student Visa — What You Need to Know

Studying in China requires a student visa, different from the 30-day visa-free entry:

  • X1 Visa — for programs 6 months or longer; requires a Residence Permit within 30 days of arrival
  • X2 Visa — for programs under 6 months

Required documents:

  • JW202 or JW201 admission notice from your university
  • Valid passport (6+ months validity)
  • Health certificate (physical exam)
  • Completed visa application form

Processing: 2–4 working days at the Chinese Embassy in Ulaanbaatar (Olympic St 7A).

Important: After arrival, register with the local police within 30 days. Your university's international student office will guide you through this.

03 — Apps Before You Leave
Apps to Install Before Leaving Mongolia
  • VPN — ExpressVPN or NordVPN, install and test in Mongolia (cannot download in China)
  • WeChat — register with Mongolian number, add Mongolian bank card
  • Alipay International — add your Visa/Mastercard
  • Amap (高德地图) — China's best navigation app, works without VPN
  • eSIM or plan to get SIM at airport — you'll need a local number for verification codes
  • DiDi — register before entering China for easier setup

05 — HSK Chinese Test
Learning Chinese — HSK Levels and How Long It Takes

Most Chinese-program universities require HSK4 or HSK5. Here's how long each level typically takes:

  • HSK1–3 (basic) — 6 months to 1 year of study
  • HSK4 (intermediate) — 1.5–2 years total
  • HSK5–6 (advanced) — 3+ years

Free apps: HSK Online, Anki (flashcards), Pleco (dictionary)

Paid apps: HelloChinese, ChinesePod

07 — Dorm Life
Dorm Tips for International Students in Shanghai
  • Some dorms have full kitchens; some have only microwaves; some have none — email the accommodation office before arrival
  • Dorm rules are strict: curfews, no overnight guests, quiet hours
  • Trash separation is mandatory in Shanghai — wet, dry, recyclable, hazardous
  • Many dorms have common rooms, laundry, and study spaces
  • Off-campus apartments: cheaper but more responsibility; budget ¥2,500–5,000/month
08 — What to Bring From Mongolia
Items Worth Bringing From Mongolia

Some things are hard to find or expensive in Shanghai — bring these from home:

  • Medicine — familiar brands you trust (painkiller, cold/flu, stomach)
  • Mongolian food — борц (bortz/dried meat), ааруул, сүүтэй цай mix — for homesickness
  • Jams salt — Chinese salt tastes different
  • Chocolate — Alpen Gold and Mongolian favorites are not in Shanghai
  • Winter clothes — Shanghai winters are cold and damp; bring a proper warm coat
09 — Cultural Tips
Understanding Chinese Culture in Shanghai

Some behaviors that might surprise first-time Mongolian visitors:

  • Loud conversations in public are completely normal — not considered rude
  • Tossing items to hand them to someone — normal gesture, not disrespectful
  • Dorm and campus rules are enforced strictly — follow them
  • Trash separation is strictly enforced in Shanghai — learn the rules early
  • Phone calls in public spaces (metro, restaurants) are common and accepted
  • Elders are shown great respect — learn basic polite forms of address
  • Tipping is not expected in Chinese restaurants
